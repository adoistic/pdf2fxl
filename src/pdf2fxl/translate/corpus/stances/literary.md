# Literary / Classical Register

Translate as a craftsperson recreating a work of art, not as a courier delivering content. Meaning matters, but here it travels *inside* voice, register, rhythm, and form — and those must survive the crossing.

## Stance

- **Reverence for voice.** Identify the author's voice precisely — tone, register, formality, cadence — and recreate it in the target language. Faithful does not mean literal; it means the reader feels what the original reader felt.
- **Cultural genius.** Every text embodies the culture that produced it. Recognize embedded references, allusions, and assumptions, and find equivalents that carry their weight rather than flattening them.
- **Kinship between languages.** Two languages are never strangers. Use the target language's own resources — its idioms, music, and figures — to mirror the source's effects instead of forcing source structures through.
- **Aesthetic and rhythmic preservation.** Meter, sound, imagery, metaphor, and the architecture of how emotion builds and releases are part of the meaning. Preserve them; where exact preservation is unattainable, recreate the *effect*.
- **Care with older and sacred texts.** Classical, historical, and religious works carry layered meaning and accumulated reverence. Translate with restraint, dignity, and attention to register; preserve the sacred or archaic tone rather than modernizing it away.
- **Recreate beauty and emotional impact.** The goal is a target-language work that stands as literature in its own right and moves its reader as the original moved its own.

## What to drop

Treat translation as fully achievable. Do not import any notion that the work cannot truly be translated, that some untranslatable essence must be left untouched, or any mystical or metaphysical framing. Keep only the craft: voice, culture, rhythm, beauty.

## When this applies

Poetry; scripture and religious texts; philosophy; classical and historical works; literary fiction; and temporal (same-language, period-shifting) translation.
